The 7 Day Coffee Fast: A Short Guide to Resetting Your Caffeine Tolerance

At 4 cups a day, something had to change.

I was inhaling idiotic amounts of the brown liquid of the gods—known to us mortals as le coffee.

It was deep frying my adrenals. And my wallet.

That’s when I found the calculus of caffeine consumption—a short piece on optimal caffeine use by Princeton CS professor Arvind Narayanan.

The basics:

  • Caffeine gives you focus by blocking adenosine (a sleep chemical).
  • The change is temporary. Our body adapts by making more.

Narayanan writes:

Over the long term, consistent caffeine consumption is as good as non-consumption, because of (you guessed it) tolerance.

So, should we just give up drinking coffee altogether?

Not quite.

Welcome to the 7 Day Coffee Fast. The technique is simple: spend one week a month with little or no caffeine consumption.

Why a week? Narayanan writes:

“With complete abstinence, it takes 5 days to reach adenosine normality; conservatively, and with imperfect abstinence, a week or 10 days may be required.”

A week lets you (and by you, I mean me) get away with drinking decaf.

Here’s what I did:

  1. I kept all of my routines and habits the same.
  2. I replaced regular coffee with decaf.

Less change means more adherence.

This caffeine deload fits well into natural human work cycles.

Or, as Narayanan puts it in big English:

“For most people, work involves a natural cyclic pattern of crunches and lean periods, and moderated coffee consumption to reflect this pattern will let you enjoy its cognition-enhancing effects more-or-less permanently.“

You hear the man. It’s a permanent solution to caffeine tolerance.

When you plan your next month, pick a down week and schedule a 7 day coffee fast. There’ll be a temporary dip in output, but you’ll see massive improvements in the long term.

Your work (and your adrenals) will thank you for it.
