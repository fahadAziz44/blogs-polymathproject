Some Lessons From 2 Years of Daily Writing

I’ve been writing for 2 years now.

Every day, no matter where I am, I wake up and write.

Here are some things I’ve learned along the way.

If you aren’t writing every day, you’re lying to yourself

A lot of my friends say they want to be writers. But when I ask about it, all I get are excuses.

“Well, I’ve been meaning too, but I’m too busy with [useless activity X]…”

Bullshit. Talk is cheap.

If you want to be a writer, you have to write. Tired? Write. Hungover? Write. Out with friends? Run into the bathroom and write.

Wake up, drink a glass of water and get at it. Smart small. Don’t start with 4 hours. Or even 1 hour. Start with one sentence.

If you can’t write a sentence every day, you don’t want to be writer.

It’ll always be scary

Not writing is safe.

Not writing means no judgment. No criticism. No laughter.

But not writing means…no writing.

And that’s much worse.

Every time I look at the publish button, I start to think: What if it sucks? What if nobody reads it? The voices are quieter now. But they never go away.

Nobody has to see your shit

The real reason we get writer’s block: we’re scared.

You don’t have to publish from day 1. Or day 100. It took me two years to publish anything. And that’s just on the internet.

I get it. Writing is hard. But remember: nobody has to see your writing but you.

Start small. Keep a journal. Put words in it. Don’t even re-read. After a while, the fear lessens. Then you can take another step. Maybe do some editing. One more step. Maybe show some friends. Then another step…

You’ll get there.

What are you sacrificing?

We can have anything we want. But not everything. Somebody famous said that. I forgot who.

What will you sacrifice for the privilege to write?

I don’t drink. I live frugally. I’m alone most mornings. Most afternoons too. I say no to parties. I don’t make a lot of money.

And I’d do it all gain.

Writing is unlearning

School makes for bad writers.

In school, we learn to use facts, not feelings. We learn to chase metrics, not moments. We learn to tell and not to show.

All in the name of ‘education’.

And now, as writers, we have to unlearn it all.

I’m still no good . But I’m unlearning every day.

‘Just write more’ is NOT good advice

Writers suck at giving writing advice. It’s a universal truth.

Common advice #1: “just write more.” Bullllshittt. One of my favorite bloggers, Tynan, has been writing for 7 years. Five books later, his writing still sucks. Love you, Tynan.

Just writing isn’t enough. There has to be process. Failure. Feedback. Chunking. “Natural” writers do this instinctively and then pass on bad advice.

Common advice #2: “just read more.” Okay. Yes, reading makes you better. But there’s two kinds of reading: active reading and passive reading. In one case, you’re just inhaling words. In the other, you’re participating. You’re asking questions like: “What is the structure?”, “Why did the author do this?”, “What would I do in his or her place?”

Both of these are “reading”. But the outcomes are entirely different.

Learn a bit about learning—it goes a long way.

A lot of writing happens away from the page

There was an interview with Seth Godin. The interviewer asked, “How much time do you spend writing?” His answer: “Oh, you mean typing? Maybe 15 minutes.”

Writing isn’t just what happens on screen. Or on paper.

A lot of it is in the head. My best ‘writing’ happens during long walks. Most days I spend several hours walking. That’s when the ideas come.

When I something pops into mind, I talk to myself. If you can’t express it out loud, you probably can’t write it. If I can’t voice it, I’ll try to figure out why. If that doesn’t work, then I’ll file it away for another day.

Clear writing is clear thinking.

Follow the rules. Then break the rules.

Rules exist for one reason: to protect idiots from themselves.

And when we start out, we’re all idiots. So follow the rules. Read Strunk’s book. Edit religiously. Ask teachers for feedback. Take classes.

Learn to be normal. Learn to be average.

Then start breaking the rules.
