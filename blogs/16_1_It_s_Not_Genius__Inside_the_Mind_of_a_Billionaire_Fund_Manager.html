It’s Not Genius: Inside the Mind of a Billionaire Fund Manager

Stanley Druckenmiller is a legend of the finance world.

A small investment of $1000 when his fund opened would have grown to $2.6 million over the next 30 years.

Druckenmiller is best known as right-hand man to George Soros—the most celebrated trader and hedge fund manager in all of history. In 1992, Soros made $1 billion in a single day with his bet the British pound sterling. Druckenmiller was right there next to him.

How did Druckenmiller achieve his success?

Perhaps he was born with it?

Nope.

It’s Not Genius

“I’m no genius. I was not in the top 10 percent of my high school class. My SATs were so mediocre I went to Bowdoin because it was the only school that didn’t require SATs, and it turned out to be a very fortunate event for me.”

I’ve seen this across top-level thinkers in many fields. Test scores and grades in school are not a good predictor of success. Actually, good grades can be a countersignal.

Traditional schooling is fixed and inorganic. To reach the top 1% of standardized testing requires you to play by house rules (and have a high tolerance for the boring). This transfers poorly to the real world.

If you want to find the next generation’s true leaders, don’t search the highest layer.

Genetics and raw intellect get too much attention; there’s a lot more at play here.

Let’s dig in…

1. Have Incredible Passion

“The thought that every event in the world affects some security price somewhere I just found incredibly intellectually [stimulating] to try and figure out what the next puzzle was and what was going to move what.”

You often hear stories of fund managers working 100+ hour weeks. Even more incredibly, these guys are doing this out their own volition. No time cards, no hourly salary – just pure obsession. And they enjoy every moment.

You do not get ahead by doing things half-assed.

2. Find Great Mentors

“If you’re early on in your career and they give you a choice between a great mentor or higher pay, take the mentor every time. It’s not even close.” 

Too many people make the mistake of not investing in human capital.
No intelligent investor would take short term money over a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity. But young ones do this all the time. The good guys do not. Warren Buffett went to his mentor, Benjamin Graham. Likewise, Stanley Druckenmiller went to George Soros.
If you are young (or old), the best thing you can do is invest in yourself.

3. Go for the Jugular 

“If you really see [an opportunity], put all your eggs in one basket and watch the basket very carefully.”

Humans are naturally risk averse. In the jungle, risk aversion works: see lion and run away. Unfortunately, these instincts don’t carry well to the modern world.

We are always finding ways to get “job stability” or “reduce stress”. Common wisdom says, “never put all your eggs in one basket”. We spread ourselves thin, cultivate a fear of failure and cease to believe in ourselves.

Then, when the opportunity of a lifetime comes, we let it go…

Don’t make that mistake. Learn to make hard decisions. Then, when the time comes, make them.

The Takeaways

Here are the takeaways again:

  1. Be obsessed with what you do.
  2. Pass on the cash. Go study with your heroes instead.
  3. If you see an opportunity, don’t be afraid to “go for the jugular”.

You might have hundreds of ideas in a year. Only two or three those will be any good. Learn to “filter” ideas into tiers of bad (avoid), decent (small time investment) and great (go all out). Allocate your time accordingly. Profit 🙂
