Derek Sivers on Conferences and Networking

I went through a bunch of Derek Sivers’s interviews and extracted the parts about networking, conferences and relationship management.

If you don’t know about Derek, he was a former professional musician that sold his company CD Baby for $22 million back in 2008.

He’s also really, really good at managing his network.The transcripts of all of his interviews are available here on his website, free for the taking.

When there’s something important that 9 out of 10 people suck at (networking, in this case), being good at it gives you a tremendous advantage.

Note: Not all of the notes come from Derek Sivers. I’ve been adding my own touches and updating this as I can.

Why conferences?

When you’re starting out and don’t have a lot of contacts, these are a great way to meet like-minded people to collaborate in the future.

Are conferences worth the price?

Figure it out by calculating conference value.

Tim Ferriss sold his first NYT bestseller (the 4HWW) mainly from live networking with A-list bloggers at conferences like SXSW.

Here’s his thinking:

  • Say the lifetime value of a subscriber is $2
  • Say a guest post with an A-list blogger is worth 200 new subscribers
  • Each successful follow-up is worth $2 x 200 = $400
  • If the costs for a conference are $2000, you only need to succeed 5 times (out of hundreds of new connections)

Still too poor? See this article on how to get into conferences for free.

How to find conferences

  • Google fu – search for “best conferences in NICHE NAME”. For example, “best conferences in tech” would give you SXSW, TechCrunch, etc.
  • Ask on popular Q&A sites like Reddit or Quora in the niche of your choice (tech, academia, etc.)

How to work a conference

Derek Sivers—

“There’s no reason to spend a single dollar or a minute of your life attending a conference, unless you do the work of walking up to strangers and getting to know them. Listening to them and not just using them as a sounding board for you to spew your crap into, but actually, you know, opening your mind and ears and listening to them and getting to know them and then following-up afterwards. If you don’t do that, it’s a total waste of time.”

1. Break the ice

This is the hardest part. But this is why you can succeed. Having the ability to go up to people and say “Hi” gives you a tremendous advantage.

2. Get their info

See the notes on conversation skills elsewhere.

3. Followup (IMPORTANT)

You might get 100 business cards from a single conference, but it doesn’t matter AT ALL if you don’t follow up. That’s $1000 dollars blown.

9 times out of 10, people don’t do the follow-up.

Derek Sivers—

“I think the follow-up is just everything. […] I think I started going to conferences when I was 19 or 20, I would just go to every conference I could and I would meet everybody I could and the most important thing is I would take every single business card back to my hotel room that night and type it all into my computer and send them a follow-up email [with a memorable comment] to kind of help them remember who I am.
