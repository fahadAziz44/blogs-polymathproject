How to Write a Book: The Strategist’s Approach

I’ve decided. I’m writing a book.

Readers of this blog know that—for any big project—I always study major successes and failures first to search for common threads.

This project is no exception. I’m not one to weave a random idea into a book, throw it out into the Internet and then pray it sells.

Today, I’m studying a recent self-publishing success: Taylor Pearson’s The End of Jobs.

Here are some of Taylor’s results in the first 6 months after his booked launched:

  • 5,000 copies sold in the 1st month, with 30,000 copies in circulation in the first 6 months
  • #1 Amazon Best Seller (in Business and Money Category)
  • $60k+ in revenue from book sales

To make matters more impressive, Taylor wrote, marketed and launched his book in 9 months. He had no significant audience or funding to start.

How did he do this?

Let’s dig in…

Structure of a Book Launch

The 9 months of effort prior to Taylor’s book launch can be roughly divided into the following 3 phases:

  1. Draft phase (writing only)
  2. Editing phase (50/50 writing and marketing)
  3. Marketing phase (marketing and branding only)

Notably, the earliest phase is writing-centric. This shifts more and more to the marketing side as the launch draws near.

Let’s look at each of the phases in more detail.

1. The Draft Phase

The main goals of this phase:

  • Define a book idea. You should have a clear problem you are trying to solve BEFORE writing.
  • Write a (shitty) first draft that is entirely driven by word count
  • Get feedback from your inner circle (2-5 close friends)

The writing tool Taylor used:

  • Scrivener — A writing app designed for longer books. Let’s you sell overall and daily word count goals.

2. The Editing Phase

Upon entering this phase, Taylor split his efforts between marketing and writing. The mornings (his “prime hours”) were spent writing while the afternoons were spent on marketing efforts.

Taylor’s editing phase was divided into 3 editing sessions. Each session was 4 weeks long.

The structure of each session:

  • Take feedback from alpha readers
  • Do a structural edit (reorganizing chapters, etc.) using reader feedback
  • Do a ‘writerly’ edit (improving prose, etc.) using reader feedback

At the end of three editing sessions, Taylor had a finished draft that was ready to go to beta readers and his editor.

Taylor also began his marketing campaign at this time. The most important lessons from Taylor’s approach is how he “baked in” the marketing to his book launch.

Some tactics that stood out to me:

  • He interviewed many people for his book. These people naturally went on to promote the launch.
  • He used blog posts to create a narrative around his book (behind the scenes look at the writing process, etc.)
  • He directly published parts of draft chapters to test for traction, build anticipation and get direct reader feedback
  • He set up a “tribe” via a landing page and Facebook group. These tribe members would go on to provide a large portion of his initial Amazon reviews.

3. The Launch Phase

This is the most tactical of the three phases.

Concentrating  all sales in the first few days of launch is crucial for reaching “escape velocity”—the state where sales begin to come naturally.

Taylor promoted the launch through three major channels:

  • Guest posts
  • Podcasts
  • Thunderclap.it — Allows supporters to schedule tweets / shares to launch all at once.

He set these up to launch at the same time (immediately upon book release). This tactic leverages the recency effect: a coordinated launch appears more significant than a spread out one.

A few other important tactics he used:

  • He offered the book for free for the first five days of the launch. Everyone who signed up pre-launch also got a free copy.
  • He gave pre-launch copies out early so that readers would have time to read and write reviews.

The Takeaways

Taylor’s shows what is possible when a first-time author with limited funding and network and accomplish with a tactical approach.

Two more takeaways:

  • There are a lot more things that Taylor could have done to “optimize” his launch. But he didn’t need to. All you need to do is (1) get a few important things right and (2) avoid the mistakes that 95%+ of authors make.
  • None of this matters if your book idea sucks. The purpose of a launch is exactly that—to give an idea the momentum it needs to stay afloat. No amount of launching is going to keep a rock in the air.
