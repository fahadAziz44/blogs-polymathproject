Bootstrapping Your Business? Do It in Chiang Mai, Thailand (Plus: A Sample Budget and Resource List)

It’s my second time in Chiang Mai, Thailand, and I’ve decided I’m coming back every year.

The city is a bootstrapper’s paradise. If you’ve got an idea but no money and no network, I can’t think of a better place to get started. A lot of the wild success this blog has seen in the last two months (over 3000 subscribers!) has been fueled by the Chiang Mai environment.

Here are a few reasons why Chiang Mai is so good for bootstrapping:

  • First world quality of life at third world prices (for $800 a month, I get what would cost $4000+ at home)
  • Fast, quality internet
  • Cheap automation of everyday tasks (laundry, cleaning, cooking, etc.)
  • A large community of creatives and freelancers (it’s the #1 hub in Asia)
  • Real espresso (not that brown water we drink in America)

By outsourcing the non-essentials, I’ve set up my life here so that I only need to freelance for 4 hours a week to cover expenses.

The rest of my time goes towards crazy experiments, many of which I share in my newsletter, The Open Circle. Or, when I’m feeling lazy, I can take a week off without worry over paying bills.

Some stuff I’ve outsourced for next to no cost in Chiang Mai:

  • Cleaning: Many apartments in Chiang Mai come with cleaning 4 times a month.
  • Cooking: The city has cheap cuisine from every country imaginable. Most meals are in the $1-5 range. I cook 95% of my meals when I’m in say, Tokyo, but never in Chiang Mai.
  • Laundry: Drop off our clothes and have them washed, dried, pressed and folded within 24 hours.
  • Photography: Get professional shots for your business at 25% of prices back home.
  • Health: First world medical treatment at a fraction of the cost (and time)! This includes blood work, dental treatment, surgery and even the cosmetic stuff (if that’s your thing).

Set up these systems and free the time to focus on what matters: growing your business (or your projects).

A Sample Monthly Budget For Chiang Mai, Thailand

The most frugal of people can live on ~$500-600. I find my personal sweet spot is around $800, but some people (especially if you enjoy night life) might spend quite a bit more.

Here’s what an actual a month of expenses looks like:

  • Housing: $200 for a studio apartment with a/c, cleaning and utilities included (and the best coffee shop in town built into it)
  • Phone: $12 for 3GB of 4G data plus a phone number (I don’t use this: I just keep my Google Fi plan because I want my US number)
  • Food: $300 to eat out well for 3-5 meals a day (I eat a lot)
  • Coffee: $90 for 2 cups a day of excellent, barista-made coffee. (Most) coffee shops come with fast wifi so you can just grab your coffee and get to work.
  • Gym Membership: $30 for an excellent gym with free weights, bars, treadmills (ugh), gymnastic rings, etc.
  • Transportation: <$50 (I mostly walk and take taxis, but you can rent a motorbike for $60)
  • Visa: $60-$100 (There are 3 main options here: visa on arrival, tourist visa or education visa.)

Total cost: about $750

To give you an idea of what prices are like, here are some other amenities you might consider:

  • Full body massage (2 hours): $15
  • Coworking space: $99 a month
  • BBQ buffets: $5-10
  • A 3-day outdoor rock climbing course: $110
  • Cooking classes: $20

There really isn’t anything I’ve seriously wanted for here. If anything, I start missing some of the nice things when I’m not here.

Interested in getting started? I’ve also included a list of the resources to help you get step up in Chiang Mai.

Chiang Mai Entrepreneur Resource List

Housing

Don’t book housing on the internet—most good places aren’t listed. And AirBnB is overpriced.

Here’s what to do instead:

  • Book a hotel for 1-2 weeks (you can find good ones for under $15 a night). Use TripAdvisor to find hotels.
  • Get a feel for the major areas in Chiang Mai (the Old City, Nimman, etc.). Wikitravel and Foursquare are a good reference.
  • Select the areas most suited for your goals. (This will most likely be the trendy, upscale Nimman area—most of the entrepreneurs and expats stay here.)
  • Spend a day shortlisting apartments in those areas
  • Make the booking.

If you’re bootstrapping with a whole team, you might want to consider renting an entire house. You can get one that fits 4 people a little bit outside of the city for $300 a month.

Work Spaces / Coworking

There are coffee shops equipped with wifi every few feet in Chiang Mai, so you’ll never have to worry about finding a place to work.

If you want a more focused, work-specific environment, here are a few candidates:

  • Punspace. The premier coworking space in Chiang Mai, with two locations. Lots of people working on interesting projects.
  • MANA. A small coworking space run by a couple. If you prefer a quieter environment, you might like this one.
  • Camp. Giant library cafe open 24 hours. It’s popular both with entrepreneurs and the local students.

Communities / Networking

Generally speaking, there are three expat communities in Chiang Mai. There are the hippy types, the retired-for-life types and the “digital nomad” entrepreneur types.

Here are two strategies for meeting the third group:

  • Go to coworking spaces. The environment in these places, especially Punspace, is quite open to chatter. Many of them will also hold weekly events where you can meet people.
  • Be active in Facebook communities. If you search for “Chiang Mai X” and replace X with your profession of interest (entrepreneurs, writers, photographers, foodies) you can find a large population of like-minded people to start collaborating with.

Other Things (Flights, Visas, etc.)

I’ll spare you the details about flights, visa arrangements and whatnot. A lot of that is available with a quick google search, and it’s not rocket science. Everybody in Chiang Mai has worked out how to deal with it (some of them for decades). It’s confusing at first, but it just becomes part of the life.

A Word on Mental Limiters

I was chatting with my friend Rob (who writes for MakeUseOf) about why the atmosphere in Chiang Mai is so ideal. It’s a combination of two factors, I think: (1) the environment and (2) the type of people the environment attracts.

The people looking for nightlife, good food and general pleasure-seeking can get it elsewhere in Thailand (Bangkok, Phuket). Only those looking to do great work come to Chiang Mai.

Chiang Mai naturally ‘selects’ for people who have that entrepreneurial streak. People who buy that first plane ticket to come here have removed their ‘mental limiters’. It’s not money that stops people; it’s fear.
