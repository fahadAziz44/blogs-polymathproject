The Completely Legal Guide to Book Pirating

Last year, I read over 150 books.

At retail prices, that many books would have easily cost me over 1000 dollars.

Books are knowledge and knowledge is pricess—or so they say. But what if you don’t have the funds?

Is pirating the only choice?

Not quite.

Over the years, I’ve come up with quite a few tricks for getting cheap books while staying on the right side of the law.

Let me share my favorite ones.

Kindle Unlimited

Amazon’s Kindle Unlimited program offers unlimited free books a month for a small fee.

Some of my favorite books are on KU, but there are two downsides to the program: (1) the number of high quality books is limited and (2) there is a lot of noise in the form of self-published garbage.

What I recommend you do:

  1. Sign up for a Kindle Unlimited free trial
  2. Read the best books that interest you (and take notes)
  3. Cancel your membership

Here are some of my favorites on Kindle Unlimited:

  • Superhuman by Habit
  • Gateless
  • Choose Yourself

Overdrive

This tool is a lifesaver.

A lot of people don’t know this, but most public libraries have large digital book collections.

Overdrive lets you check out ebooks and audiobooks for free using your local library card. The books disappear after the loan expires…but you get to keep all your notes!

If you don’t have a local card, there are some libraries where you can pay a small yearly fee (<$50) to access their collections.

Audible

The king of audiobook platforms. For $15 a month, you get 1 credit that you can trade for any audiobook.

That’s a decent deal, but pricey if you are on the budget.

Here’s some stuff I’ve done to save money:

  • Sign up for a free trial to get 2 free audiobooks.
  • Cancel your membership and select “too expensive” for the reason—you’ll get an offer for 50% off 3 months of membership.
  • “Freeze” your account and wait for special Audible sales, where bestsellers can go for heavy discounts.

I listen to a lot of audio, and I’d be lost without Audible. This is one of the tools where it’s worth it to go ahead and pay the subscription. There are quite a few resources that aren’t available in any other format. For a real treat, check out Famous Greeks—the whole 12 hours are a clinic in masterful storytelling.

Project Gutenberg

The majority of books “worth re-reading” are old books. They’ve survived for a reason, after all.

Project Gutenberg offers these old, copyright free books for free download. The books come in all the popular formats (mobi, epub, pdf, txt, etc.).

Here’s a link to the top 100 most downloaded books.

If you’re lazy like me, you can download direct from Amazon by doing a search for Kindle classics that are priced at $0.00.

Bookfinder

Bookfinder is the best tool out there for finding used copies of physical books.

It does a blanket search of all the used book distributors out there (Amazon, Abebooks, etc.) and sorts them by lowest price.

Many good books can be found at the $3-$5 price range, with shipping included!

—

That’s it—the 5 tools I’ve used to get free or massively discounted books.

One caveat: If you can at all afford it, I suggest allocating $50-$100 a month to a “self-education” budget. By forcing yourself to spend money, you’ll reinforce the importance of learning in your mind (severely undervalued).

Happy reading!
