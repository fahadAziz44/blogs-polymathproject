$1000 Publishing Challenge: Review & Lessons Learned

EDIT: The $1000 Publishing Challenge is Now Finished! The results and lessons learned are below. Scroll down further for the initial challenge rules.

Challenge Details

In late 2016, I bet my readers $1000 that I could publish every day for 30 days straight. The main motivation of the challenge was to conquer my fear of publishing.

Results

The good stuff:

  • I successfully completed 30/30 days of the challenge
  • Over 4000 new readers joined my weekly newsletter, The Open Circle. This more than doubled readership from before the challenge started.
  • Two of my popular articles were syndicated to the New York Observer.
  • I syndicated many of the same articles to Medium, earning over 140,000 reads and several thousand followers.

The bad stuff:

  • Overall post quality dropped from the pressure of publishing every day. But, despite my fear, my readers didn’t all abandon me and stop reading.
  • The time I spent on writing took away from other projects.

What I Learned

  • I’m less afraid of the publish button. The main goal of the challenge was to get over the fear of making my writing public. Before I did this, I thought that a single bad post would send readers scrambling to block me for life. I imagined my inbox flooded with hate mail. I got a bit of hate mail, but most of the stuff was positive.
  • I have a better idea of my real writing output. When I designed this challenge, I was genuinely worried whether I could write 500 words a day. Now I know that I can pump out 2000+ words in a single hour if I have the right conditions set up.
  • I found my optimal writing hours. There seem to be two ideal writing blocks for me: (1) the first 2-3 hours of the morning and (2) the last 2-3 hours before deadline.
  • I discovered the importance of environment. For me, the ideal conditions for a large number of good ideas are: (1) a space with other people, (2) no WiFi access and (3) boredom. My highest output day actually happened on a flight to Singapore.
  • Always outline first. Even 10 minutes spent writing it out can save hours in failed pieces and dead ends later.

How I Would Change Things

I think the challenge was 90% a success.

If I were to repeat the challenge, here are a few tweaks I would make:

  • Set the “end of day” cutoff earlier. I set mine to midnight. That was a bad idea for sleep. If I could restart, I would set it to 8 pm.
  • Set a lower word count. The point of the challenge is to press publish. It’s okay to write a shorter piece. There were several times where I said everything I needed to say, but hadn’t hit 500 words. I was forced to either add “fluff” or discard the piece.
  • Consider banning quotes. If you set a word goal of 500, it’s easy to just get a bunch of quotes from other sources to help meet your word count. I did this several times.
  • Write in two sessions. On the best days, I wrote the draft first and then went to do something else. I then did a second session with editing.

Onwards to the next challenge!

Original Challenge Post

I hate the publish button.

Click it…and whoosh, your work is naked to thousands of judging eyes. It’s damn scary. So, I write and don’t publish. I kill 4 out of 5 of my drafts—they never live to see the click.

But it’s not my writing that needs killing. It’s something else.

It’s procrastination.

It’s high time I crushed this fear of public failure. To do this, I’m making myself an offer I can’t refuse: If I can’t publish 500 words a day for 30 days, I will pay you $1000.

That’s right, I’m asking you, my reader, to help kill my fear of publishing.

Interested? I’ve drafted the challenge rules below.

Note: If you’re only interested in the psychological backing and experiment design, skip down to the FAQ section.

The Rules

  • Every day, before noon EST (which is midnight here in Thailand), I will publish a post on my Facebook page with a link to something I published that day.
  • This can be on my blog or for any other platform I write on (guest post, magazines, Medium, Quora, etc.)
  • The post has to be at least 500 words.
  • If I fail to post on any day for the next 30 days (Nov 15 – Dec 14, 2016), I will pay $1000 to whoever catches me.

How to Take My Money

  • If I don’t provide a link to a published article on my Facebook page by noon EST, message me (Facebook or email) telling me I’ve failed.
  • I’ll send the money at random to one of the people who catch me within the first 24 hours. (But I’m probably not going to fail)

Note: You must have a PayPal account to be eligible. Also, I reserve the right the void my own punishment in the event of (1) family emergency and (2) extreme personal illness.

FAQ

Why are you doing this?

A few reasons:
* I want to make a statement about human possibilities. With the correct approach humans are capable of way more than the average person believes.
* I want to crush my fear of publishing (aka fear of failure).
* I want to “upgrade” my consistent writing habit (which I’ve been doing for almost 3 years now) into a consistent publishing habit.
* I get a dozen new ideas for each article I publish, and this has built a giant backlog of post ideas. I’d like to start tackling some of these.

Why did you design the challenge in this way?

The experiment uses two powerful psychological drivers: loss aversion (losing $1000) and social pressure (letting down my readers AND looking stupid). Basically, I’ve raised the financial and social stakes so high that I wouldn’t ever want to fail.

How do you have the time for this?

I’ve set up my life so that I only spend less than 1 hour a day on income generation. The rest of the my time goes to passion projects and silly experiments.

Are you rich?

No, $1000 is 5-6 weeks of living costs for me (I spend $700-$800 a month here in Thailand). But for this challenge to work, I need to make the risks real.

Will you actually pay me if you fail?

Yes, but you’ll just have to take my word for it.
