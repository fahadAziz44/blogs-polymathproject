How to Self-Publish and Hit the Bestseller Lists

What does it take for self-publishers to hit the bestsellers lists?

I mentioned recently that I’m thinking of self-publishing a book.

Readers thought it was a good idea.

So I’m probably going to write one. And if I’m going to write one, of course I’m going to aim for the bestseller lists.

To do that, I need to understand how the system works.

These aren’t just notes—they’re also a look at how I might reverse engineer a particular objective. A blind coin flip (that lands on tails 99% of the time) is not the only way to do a book launch.

The book will happen 9+ months in the future, but the framework gets build now.

What that means is I’m:

  1. Taking loads of notes on the subject
  2. Doing a feasibility analysis (Are people interested? How much will it cost (time and money)? How soon can I publish? What is the industry like? What are the big mistakes? etc.)
  3. Drafting up a plan

Let’s dig in…

What are the major lists? How are rankings determined?

The first thing to realize is that the term ‘bestselling’ is a lie. You can sell 10x what books on the list sell and not make the list.

Let’s look at each of the top 4 lists.

The New York Times

  • Separated into 20 weekly and 3 monthly lists
  • They are elitist and selective. Rankings are calculated with private “polling” of a proprietary list of publishers
  • The selection is particularly harsh for “advice” nonfiction books
  • Ebook sales not counted for those nonfiction books

Takeaway: This makes it IMPOSSIBLE to make the list with a self-published book. The NYT is off our radar.

The Wall Street Journal

  • Weekly list
  • Lists split into fiction, nonfiction and business
    • Separate lists for physical and ebook
  • They get fair data from all major physical and digital sellers (except Apple) using Nielsen BookScan reporting
  • They exclude e-books that are free or less than $0.99
  • Audiobooks are excluded

Takeaway: Much fairer than the Times. We can just focus on sales without worrying about funny business.

USA Today

Least prestigious of the three lists.

  • Weekly list
  • Lumps all books together into one category.
  • If you hit the Wall Street Journal list, you’ll probably hit this.
  • Counting goes from Monday to Sunday

Takeaway: We can ignore this and focus on WSJ only—they use similar metrics and we will likely hit both at the same time.

Amazon

  • Based purely off of Amazon sales
  • Private algorithm that nobody knows about
  • Rankings determined off hourly sales
    • It’s counterproductive to concentrate sales in the same hour. It will drop rankings in other hour frames.

Takeaway: Amazon rankings are only affected by Amazon sales. But the reverse is not true—Amazon sales still affect WSJ rankings.

What does it take to hit the lists?

  • The New York Times — To hit the list, you’ll need a publishing deal, at least 18 months and somewhere from 5,000-10,000 book sales. Remember, ebooks don’t cont.
    • This is out for self-published authors.
  • The Wall Street Journal — Get 3,000 to 5,000 sales, and you’re in. No funny business.
  • USA Today — Similar to WSJ.
  • Amazon — It takes about 2000 sales in a day to hit the Amazon Top 10. These are only sales on the Amazon platform.

How to Hit the Lists

With the above information in hand, it’s clear what it takes to hit all of the bestseller lists.

  1. Concentrate on Amazon sales
  2. Launch the book on Monday or Tuesday
  3. Sell 5000 books in the first week
    • Plan for failures by setting milestones for 10-20k sales

That’s it. Two steps.

That’s all for the self-publishing notes.

For the next stage, I’ll be looking at how to realistically get 10-20k preorders on Amazon.

Some questions I’ll be asking:

  • How big of a community do I need?
    • What percentage of ‘fans’ tend to purchase books?
  • How much traffic to A-list blogs get in a week?
    • What percentage of A-list blog readers end up purchasing books?

Note: As a very, very rough estimate, I’m looking at 100k ‘fans’ on the blog through email, social media, etc. and then another 150k of outreach during the first week of the launch.
